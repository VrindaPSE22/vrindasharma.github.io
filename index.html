Development Economics · Environmental Economics

Water, climate adaptation, and structural transformation.

I am a PhD candidate in Economics at the Paris School of Economics. My research studies how households in developing countries respond to water-related environmental risks, with a focus on climate adaptation, safe water adoption, agricultural livelihoods, and labor reallocation.

About

My work combines household survey data, spatial environmental data, field experiments, and quasi-experimental methods. I am particularly interested in settings where households face both environmental constraints and institutional frictions that shape economic choices.

My current projects study freshwater salinity in the Mekong Delta of Viet Nam, adoption of safe drinking water technologies in Gujarat, and the role of dairy cooperatives in rural structural transformation in India.

Outside research, I enjoy reading about history and archaeology and attending Indian classical music concerts.

Research

Freshwater Salinity and Structural Transformation in Viet Nam

Draft available on request

Joint with Sepehr Eslami

This paper studies how freshwater salinity affects agricultural productivity, household income, labor allocation, and migration in the Mekong Delta. I combine household panel data with spatial salinity measures from a hydrological model to examine how a sector-specific climate shock shapes local adaptation and structural transformation.

Abstract

This paper examines how freshwater salinization, a sector-specific climate shock, shapes labor reallocation and structural transformation. Using household panel data from Viet Nam’s Mekong Delta combined with spatially calibrated hydrological salinity estimates, we show that salinity generates large declines in agricultural output and releases labor from agriculture. Adjustment occurs primarily through localized reallocation into manual work and self-employment rather than through migration. Whether this reallocation translates into structural transformation depends critically on local non-agricultural absorbing capacity: wards with a substantial presence of non-agricultural industries are able to absorb displaced labor, while those without rely more heavily on remittances. Gendered reallocation is explained by wage differentials in the absorbing sector among men and women. These findings highlight how climate change can reshape rural labor markets and underscore the importance of sectoral diversity and wage equalization, even in predominantly agricultural regions.

Barriers to Safe Water Technology Adoption in Rural India

Pilot in progress

Joint with Balasai Vanukuri

This project evaluates interventions to increase adoption of community reverse osmosis plants in Gujarat. The experiment studies whether information, habit formation, and reductions in unequal collection costs can increase take-up of safe drinking water technologies at low prices.

Dairy Cooperatives and Rural Transformation in India

Work in progress

Joint with Balasai Vanukuri

This project studies how cooperative institutions and dairy market access affected rural livelihoods, infrastructure, and long-term structural transformation.

Menstrual Stigma, Hygiene, and Human Capital: Experimental Evidence from India

Pilot in progress

Joint with Karen Macours and Duncan Webb

This project studies whether interventions targeting menstrual stigma, menstrual health knowledge, and school environments can improve girls’ wellbeing and educational outcomes in India. The study examines how social norms and psychosocial barriers shape human capital accumulation during adolescence.
